# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Engineers and technical founders who already use coding agents (Claude Code, Codex, Cursor) every week. They arrive three ways: live attendees of the free Maven lightning lessons, viewers of the recordings, and prospective students evaluating the paid course. They are skeptical of AI hype and respond to evidence, working code, and honest trade-offs.

## Product Purpose

This repo is the public home of the lightning lessons around the Hardcore Agentic Engineering Maven course (August 2026 cohort). Each lesson teaches one self-contained piece of the course's argument and funnels interested attendees toward the course. Success: an attendee can run the lesson's technique the same night, and enough of them enroll.

## Brand Personality

Plainspoken, evidence-first, seaworthy. The definition-of-done deck commits to a dead-reckoning nautical chart identity: chart-water background with graticule grid, Besley serif display, IBM Plex mono chrome, magenta as the single accent, buff paper panels, phosphor terminal screens. The voice teaches judgment, not just tools ("if you're watching, you are the definition of done").

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS pitch decks: gradient heroes, icon-card grids, hockey-stick metrics.
- AI-hype content: unverifiable claims, breathless superlatives, exclamation points.
- Corporate template slides: bullet walls, stock clip art, dense agendas.

## Design Principles

1. Evidence over opinion. Every claim traces to something checkable: an exit code, a commit, a captured session.
2. Show the loop, don't describe it. Working terminals and runnable code beat diagrams of them.
3. Teach judgment, not just the hammer. Always say when NOT to use the technique.
4. The metaphor serves the lesson. Dead reckoning frames the deck; it never obscures the content.
5. Identity preservation. The deck's visual system is committed and shipping; refine within it, don't redesign it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA as the bar: body text at 4.5:1 contrast or better, keyboard navigation for all deck controls, visible focus states, and a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative for every animation. Decks are presented on projectors and in screen recordings, so legibility at low resolution matters more than usual.
